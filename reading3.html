Motherhood in crisise by Laurence Ivil, Alicia Prager and Saidu Bah

In the graphic novel "Motherhood in Crisis," Laurence Ivil, Alicia Prager, and Saidu Bah examine maternal healthcare as a force that determines survival. Although the narrative focuses on women in Sierra Leone, their stories feel universally urgent. The authors argue that high maternal mortality is not just a medical failure, but a structural one. Reading this text has made me reconsider the privilege of medical access and the hidden dangers of giving birth within a broken system.

The authors show that maternal health is often dictated by a "code" of accessibility. In the stories of Memunatu and Kadiatu, physical distance and lack of infrastructure frame what is considered a "safe" pregnancy. When I see the panels depicting their grueling treks to clinics, I realize the crisis begins long before they reach a hospital. This visual storytelling challenges clinical statistics by making the exhaustion and fear feel more real than a data point.

The novel also emphasizes that healthcare is an act of power. Every story reflects decisions by a state that has often prioritized other sectors over the lives of rural women. This made me reconsider the role of traditional medicine; as seen in Kadiatu’s narrative, traditional healers are often the only resource when the state fails. I agree with the authors’ implication that when healthcare is a luxury, motherhood becomes a performance of survival. In our fast-paced digital world, it is easy to overlook these crises, but this medium brings the reader into the immediate presence of the struggle.

Ultimately, Motherhood in Crisis has changed how I think about reproductive rights. Rather than seeing these stories as simple records of misfortune, I now understand them as tools that demand a shift in how we perceive global inequality. Moving forward, I want to be more intentional in how I consume global news, using these narratives to question the structures that allow such a crisis to persist.
