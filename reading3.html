Motherhood in crisis by Laurence Ivil, Alicia Prager and Saidu Bah

In the graphic novel Motherhood in Crisis, Laurence Ivil, Alicia Prager, and Saidu Bah examine how systemic poverty and maternal healthcare determine survival. Although the narrative focuses on women in Sierra Leone, their stories feel universally urgent. The authors argue that high maternal mortality is not just a medical failure but a structural one influenced by geography and tradition. Reading this text has made me reconsider the privilege of medical access and the hidden dangers of giving birth in a broken system.

In Memunatu’s story, the physical distance to a medical facility becomes a character in itself. The graphic medium effectively illustrates the grueling trek through difficult terrain, emphasizing that for many women the crisis begins long before they enter a hospital. This visual storytelling challenges clinical statistics by turning human struggle into an immersive experience. The art conveys a sense of isolation, where the vast spaces between the characters and the help they need highlight a profound geographic injustice.

Heela’s story shifts the focus to the intersection of education and maternal health. As a schoolgirl whose life is derailed by pregnancy, her narrative underscores how the lack of reproductive agency feeds a cycle of poverty. The illustrations capture the shrinking of her world, moving from the possibilities of a classroom to the claustrophobic reality of early motherhood. It forces the reader to confront how a single biological event can terminate a woman’s social and economic potential in a society that lacks a safety net.

In Kadiatu’s story, the narrative provides a haunting look at the emotional toll of high infant mortality. Her experience touches on the tension between traditional healing and modern medicine. Rather than vilifying traditional practices, the novel suggests these are often the only recourse for women abandoned by the state. I agree with the authors’ implication that when healthcare is a luxury, motherhood becomes a performance of survival. The tragedy lies not in the choice of a healer but in the absence of a real choice to begin with.

Tetteh’s story highlights the extreme vulnerability of women at the margins of society. When pregnancy occurs within the carceral system, the crisis of motherhood is compounded by a lack of dignity and basic human rights. Her narrative serves as a reminder that maternal health is a social justice issue that permeates every institution. This perspective pushes me to think more critically about how systemic issues, like the legal system or lack of education, relate to one another to shape a woman's worth.

Ultimately, Motherhood in Crisis has changed how I think about reproductive rights and global health. Rather than seeing these stories as simple records of misfortune, I now understand them as powerful tools that demand a shift in how we perceive and address global inequality. Moving forward, I want to be more intentional in how I consume global news, using these narratives not just to witness suffering but to question the structures that allow such a crisis to persist in the 21st century.
